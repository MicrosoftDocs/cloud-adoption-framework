# Cost and management standards for Fabric

With a unified architecture and governance guardrails in place, focus on the day-to-day practices for handling data. Operational standards ensure that every data domain follows consistent processes to move data from source systems into OneLake, transform it into high-quality data products, and make those products available for use. By standardizing these processes, you achieve a single source of truth in OneLake and make analytics and AI outcomes repeatable and trustworthy. This section covers best practices for data ingestion, processing, publishing, securing, and consuming data products.

## 1. Set cost management standards

Cost management standards define how teams control spending and optimize resource usage once Microsoft Fabric is running. This matters because uncontrolled costs can quickly erode the platform’s business value. **Recommendation:** Decision makers should establish clear cost governance processes. These should include regular executive cost reviews and defined approval rules for scaling capacity, to keep the unified data platform cost-effective and aligned with business goals. Here’s a checklist:

1. **Review platform costs and performance at the leadership level.** Regular, executive-level oversight ensures that cost and usage data actually drive decisions. **Best practices:** Hold routine leadership reviews to examine how the data platform is performing and what it is costing. Use those meetings to align spending with business priorities and address any inefficiencies. **Decision guidance:** Decide how often to hold these reviews. A quarterly review might suffice for stable operations, while a monthly cadence is better during periods of rapid growth or change. More frequent oversight catches issues early. However, it also demands more leadership time, so assign a schedule that balances benefit and effort. See [Understand your Fabric capacity Azure bill](/fabric/enterprise/azure-billing) and [Evaluate and optimize your Microsoft Fabric capacity](/fabric/enterprise/optimize-capacity)

2. **Control capacity scaling decisions.** Unplanned capacity upgrades can cause budget surprises without clear benefit. **Best practices:** Define a formal process for evaluating and approving any increase in Fabric capacity. Require teams to present justification, such as specific demand growth or performance data. Get sign-off from a designated authority (for example, a cloud governance board) before scaling up. Tie capacity increases to business needs and agreed performance targets, rather than on-the-fly reactions. **Decision guidance:** Determine the level of control needed. For major capacity changes that affect shared budgets, keep approval centralized to protect financial stability. You may allow domain teams to self-approve small adjustments once they prove they can manage costs responsibly. Central oversight lowers financial risk but can slow response. Selective autonomy speeds decisions, but grant it only when teams have demonstrated mature cost discipline. See [Pause and resume your capacity](/fabric/enterprise/pause-resume) and [Scale your Fabric capacity](/fabric/enterprise/scale-capacity).

3. **Set standards for throttling and surge scenarios**. Plan for workload spikes. Sudden surges in usage can strain resources or trigger system throttling. **Best practices:** Establish plans for how to handle peak loads. Monitor capacity and set thresholds that alert teams to impending strain, so they can scale up or optimize before users are impacted. Utilize features like auto-scaling where possible, and schedule non-urgent jobs for off-peak hours. Leaders should treat any recurring platform throttling as a problem to fix (by adding capacity or optimizing processes), not as an acceptable norm. **Decision guidance:** Decide what level of performance dip, if any, is tolerable during peak times. You might accept brief slowdowns on non-critical workloads to save cost. However, for critical services, ensure enough capacity or optimizations are in place to maintain performance even at peak. Providing extra headroom improves reliability at a higher cost. Running leaner saves money but risks user dissatisfaction during traffic spikes. See [Understand your Fabric capacity throttling](/fabric/enterprise/throttling) and [Surge protection](/fabric/enterprise/surge-protection).

By enforcing these cost standards, leaders ensure the platform stays aligned to value. The unified data platform remains reliable. It operates within expected cost boundaries. This is critical for long term success and trust in the organization’s data strategy.

## 2. Set monitoring standards

Even with a strong architecture and governance baseline, a data platform needs ongoing monitoring to stay healthy. Proactive checks help leaders catch issues early. They ensure the platform performs well and that data remains trustworthy, all while preventing surprise cost overruns. **Recommendation:** Define clear metrics and centralized dashboards to continuously monitor the data platform’s health. This includes tracking performance and reliability indicators and watching data quality and spending. Establish regular reviews of these metrics to guide optimizations and maintain trust in the platform. Here's a checklist

1. **Monitor health metrics.** Ongoing measurement of platform health is the foundation of effective operations. **Best practices:** Identify a set of core metrics for Microsoft Fabric’s performance and reliability. For instance, monitor capacity utilization and query response times as key indicators. Additionally, track error or failure rates as a gauge of data pipeline health. Use a unified dashboard, such as the [Fabric Capacity Metrics app](/fabric/enterprise/metrics-app) so that all domains are observed in one place. Review these metrics on a consistent schedule (weekly or monthly) to catch trends like rising errors or slowing queries before they become serious. **Decision guidance:** Decide who will oversee these metrics. A centralized platform operations team can provide consistency and a holistic view, but this requires dedicated resources. Conversely, assigning each domain team to monitor its own metrics can work if teams are skilled, but it risks missing organization-wide patterns. Many organizations strike a balance by centralizing the monitoring tools and standards, while involving domain teams to interpret and act on anomalies specific to their area.

2. **Centralize reliability and performance oversight.** A single view of system reliability helps reveal issues that individual teams might miss.** Best practices:** Implement unified monitoring or alerts for critical failures and performance problems across all data pipelines. For example, set up notifications if any scheduled data refresh fails or if processing time exceeds defined thresholds, and have those alerts go to a central operations channel. This way, if multiple domains experience similar issues (e.g. a pattern of slow nightly jobs), the central team can spot the systemic problem and address it holistically. **Decision guidance:** Assign responsibility for responding to these platform-wide alerts. A dedicated platform reliability team can triage incidents and coordinate cross-domain fixes, minimizing downtime at the cost of additional operational overhead. Alternatively, each domain team could manage its own pipeline reliability. This encourages autonomy, but may result in slower detection of issues that span multiple domains. In practice, many organizations benefit from a central team handling widespread incidents, while domain teams focus on their local issues and improvements. Use the [**Fabric Monitoring hub**](/fabric/admin/monitoring-hub) rather than reviewing individual workload logs.

## 3. Set deployment standards

Deployment standards define how data pipelines, transformations, and analytics are moved into production. Consistent, automated deployment processes are crucial to ensure the data platform remains stable and secure, with minimal risk from human error. **Recommendation:** Treat every data artifact (pipelines, code, queries, machine learning models, dashboards) as code. Enforce the use of source control and automated CI/CD pipelines for testing and releases. No one should manually modify production data processes; all changes must flow from a version-controlled repository through an automated pipeline.

1. **Put all data artifacts under version control.** Proper versioning is the basis of consistent deployments. **Best practices:** Manage everything in a source control repository. This includes pipelines, ETL scripts, SQL queries, configuration files, and even BI reports. A version control system creates a history of changes, supports team collaboration through code reviews or merge processes. It allows rollback if a change causes problems. **Decision guidance:** Make this a firm policy: no change goes live unless it’s committed to source control first. Some data teams might need training to adopt Git and related workflows, but it’s crucial for preventing "shadow changes" and configuration drift. This policy adds a bit of process overhead for each update. However, it improves traceability and confidence, as every production change is accountable and reversible.

**2. Use CI/CD pipelines for releases.** Automate testing and deployment to eliminate manual errors. **Best practices:** Implement a continuous integration/continuous deployment (CI/CD) pipeline that takes code from version control, runs tests in a pre-production environment, and then promotes the updates to production in a consistent, repeatable way. For example, when a data pipeline is updated, the CI/CD process should first run data quality checks or unit tests on the transformation logic in a staging area. Only if those tests pass is the new pipeline deployed into the live Fabric workspace. Automation ensures all changes are applied the same way every time, reducing mistakes and preventing ad-hoc configuration changes.**Decision guidance:** Leaders should mandate that no one deploys changes by clicking around in a GUI or running untracked scripts in production. All updates must come through the automated pipeline. This might require upfront investment in tools, like Azure DevOps or GitHub Actions, and time spent on setting up the pipeline. But it pays off by reducing errors and enabling fast recovery. You can roll back to the last stable version easily. Manual deployments might seem faster for a quick fix, but it inevitably leads to inconsistent environments and issues that are hard to debug or reproduce.
